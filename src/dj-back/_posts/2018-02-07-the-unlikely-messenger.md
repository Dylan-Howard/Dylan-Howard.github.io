<p class="quote">“Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by God’s will”</p>
<p class="quote">Ephesians 1:1a</p>
<hr>
<p>Consider how remarkable this statement is, “Paul, an apostle of Christ…”. Paul, the man who approved of brutally killing followers of Christ (Acts 8:1). Paul, the man who sought the end of Christ’s followers (Acts 8:3). Paul, who invaded the homes of Christians and tore families apart as he dragged them out and into prison (Acts 8:3).</p>
<p>This man, in all of his evident wretchedness, is now called an apostle—a messenger of God. Now, Paul’s mission has a different tune. He writes, “We have received grace and apostleship through Him to bring about the obedience of faith among all the nations, on behalf of His name” (Romans 1:5). Paul’s work has gone from destruction to construction, from inspiring apostasy to inspiring obedience.</p>
<p>How can this be? How can a man set on the destruction of God’s kingdom now be sent to build God’s kingdom? Paul’s new title and mission is rightly followed with “by God’s will.” Only by God’s glorious work of conversion could Paul undergo such a momentous shift. Only by the will of the Father—whose will far surpasses ours—could Paul ever desire this. Only by the Lord’s gracious gift of a new heart could Paul ever desire or carry out the mission of God (Ezekiel 36:26).</p>
<p>This gift of conversion is the same gift on which we rest. While our lives may not seem as openly antagonistic towards God’s throne, the Bible affirms that we are far worse than our imaginations can fathom. Our wickedness cannot be summed up in the harm it causes (though it is great). Our wickedness is in the contempt towards God in our rebellion. God described David’s adultery this way, “because you have despised me and have taken the wife of Uriah the Hittite to be your wife,” and, “by this deed you have utterly scorned the Lord” (2 Samuel 12:10, 14; Emphasis Added).</p>
<p>Despite the radicle transformation of desire, the most stunning point of this passage is that Paul would be allowed to be a messenger. Why would such a wretched man be allowed the joy of spiritual service? This is the very place we find ourselves. We who are in Christ, with Paul, are called as messengers (2 Cor. 5:12). But why? Why would the Lord allow broken people into His service? Why would God use us to establish churches and encourage them? Why would an all-powerful God use such humble means to redeem the world?</p>
<p>Why God uses us is a mystery, but what is evident in this is the Lord’s grace to us. We are allowed the deep joy of working alongside the Master Builder in constructing His Church. Indeed, this is an immeasurable joy (c.f. 1 Thessalonians 2:20). May God receive the glory as He uses imperfect creatures for a perfect mission in accordance with His perfectly good and perfectly powerful will.</p>
